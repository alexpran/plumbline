# Contributing to Plumbline

The most valuable thing you can contribute is **a field report**: you tried
Plumbline on real work, and you're telling me what happened.

This is not the usual open-source contract. Plumbline is one person's documented
practice, still finding its shape. It doesn't need a flood of pull requests — it
needs evidence from people who aren't me.

## Send a field report (the main thing)

Open a **[field report issue](../../issues/new?template=field-report.md)** after you
run any part of the method on something real. The template walks you through it, but
in short:

- What were you building, and on what stack?
- Which checks did you use? (Brief Linting, Assumption Surfacing, Omission Catalog)
- What surfaced that you'd otherwise have missed?
- What felt like pure overhead?
- What did you change or skip by instinct?
- Would you reach for it again?

Negative reports are as useful as positive ones. "I tried Brief Linting and it just
slowed me down on small tasks" is real signal. Don't soften it.

## Other ways to engage

- **Discussions** — open questions, "does this generalize to X?", half-formed ideas.
- **Issues** — friction or gaps *in the method itself*, or anything that reads as
  tied to a specific stack (it shouldn't be).

## About pull requests

Right now Plumbline is intentionally a single coherent vision, because that
coherence is most of its value at this stage. So:

- **Welcome:** typo and clarity fixes, broken links, obvious errors.
- **Open a discussion first:** anything that changes the method's direction, adds a
  check, or restructures the artifacts. Let's talk before you write it.
- **Encouraged, separately:** fork it and adapt it to your stack or team. That's a
  great use of this. If you do, a field report on what you had to change is gold.

## Tone

Be direct. This works best as a candid exchange between practitioners, not a polite
one. Critique the method hard; that's the job.
